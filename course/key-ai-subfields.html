Key AI Subfields: NLP, Computer Vision and Edge AI

AI isn't one thing. Three subfields are worth understanding specifically because they're everywhere and they do very different things. Natural language processing handles words. Computer vision handles images. Edge AI moves the processing off the cloud and onto the device. Each one matters for different reasons.

AI isn't monolithic. There are specialised subfields tackling different types of problems, and three of them show up in almost everything: natural language processing, computer vision, and edge AI. Understanding what each actually does - and where each fails - changes how you think about what's possible.

Natural Language Processing (NLP)

NLP is the subfield that handles human language. Text, speech, conversation - if it involves words, NLP is involved. It's also the most visible and most hyped subfield because it produced ChatGPT, Microsoft Copilot, and everything else text-based you've used recently.

NLP systems learn statistical patterns in language. They can translate between languages, answer questions from documents, summarise text, generate text from prompts, classify documents by topic or sentiment, and extract specific information from unstructured writing. Gmail's Smart Reply is NLP. Search engines understanding intent rather than just keywords - that's NLP. Voice assistants converting speech to meaning - NLP again.

Medical systems now scan research papers and extract relevant findings at scale, which was impossible before modern NLP. That's genuinely useful. But the limits are real. NLP systems are good at pattern matching in language and poor at understanding meaning or truth. ChatGPT can write convincingly about topics it doesn't understand. It generates false information confidently. It can't tell accurate sources from fiction if both appeared in its training data.

NLP is also still primarily designed around English. Systems for other languages exist but are generally less capable because they were trained on less data.

Computer Vision

Computer vision handles images and video. In some ways it's more mathematically mature than NLP because an image is just numerical data and you can apply well-understood operations to find patterns in it.

Computer vision systems identify objects in images, detect faces, read text from photographs, segment images into regions, estimate 3D structure from 2D images, and track movement in video. The applications are already widespread. Autonomous vehicles use computer vision to see roads, pedestrians, and other cars in varying lighting and weather. Medical imaging systems flag areas of concern in X-rays and MRIs for doctors to review. Amazon's cashier-less stores track what customers pick up. Manufacturing lines inspect products for defects faster and more consistently than humans can.

The limits matter. Computer vision is reliable on images that resemble its training data and unreliable on anything else - unusual angles, unusual lighting, objects it's never seen. It's also brittle in a specific way: adversarial examples, which are slight modifications to images that don't fool humans at all, can completely fool the AI. And image analysis is computationally expensive, which connects directly to the edge AI problem below.

See the previous lesson on deep learning for how convolutional neural networks actually do the image recognition work.

Edge AI

Edge AI means running AI on the device rather than in the cloud. Instead of sending your data to a server for processing, the device does the work locally.

Nobody hypes edge AI much, but it matters. Cloud processing has real costs: you send your data somewhere, you wait for a response, you pay for bandwidth, and if your internet connection drops, you can't process anything. Edge AI means faster responses, better privacy, and operation without internet.

Your phone's face-unlock system is edge AI - your face isn't sent to Apple's or Google's servers. Fitness watches detecting walking versus running by analysing motion on the device itself - edge AI. Industrial sensors detecting equipment anomalies locally without streaming all sensor data to a server - edge AI. Hearing aids filtering background noise in real-time on the device - edge AI.

The challenge is real: edge devices have limited computing power. The best AI models have tens or hundreds of billions of parameters. You can't run those on a phone or a watch. So edge AI requires smaller, more specialised models, which often perform less well than their large cloud-based counterparts. Companies are actively working on this - model compression, distillation, efficient architectures - because the prize is significant.

Which Subfield Will Have the Most Impact

NLP gets the attention, but I think edge AI will be more consequential over the next 5-10 years. NLP's impact is largely cultural - chat, text generation, creative tools. Computer vision is already embedded in real industrial systems and will keep improving steadily.

But edge AI is foundational in a way the others aren't. Every phone, car, watch, sensor, and piece of industrial equipment could gain AI capabilities that don't depend on cloud infrastructure. That's where the real economic impact happens - not because one model somewhere got better at writing poems, but because billions of devices can process and act on information locally.

Right now, edge AI is the bottleneck. Models are getting better but still too large for most edge hardware. Once that's solved - and people are actively solving it - you get AI capabilities distributed across everything, without the latency, cost, and privacy problems that cloud dependency brings.

Understanding all three subfields matters for working with the AI tools covered in the next lesson. Most products combine them - a voice assistant uses NLP and speech recognition; a self-driving system uses computer vision and edge processing; an enterprise chatbot uses NLP and retrieval.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is natural language processing in AI?

Natural language processing (NLP) is the AI subfield that handles human language - text, speech and conversation. NLP systems learn patterns in how language is used and can translate, summarise, classify, generate and extract information from text. ChatGPT, Copilot and voice assistants are all NLP applications.

What is computer vision used for?

Computer vision systems identify and interpret images and video. Real applications include autonomous vehicle navigation, detecting tumours in medical scans, quality control in manufacturing, and tracking what customers pick up in cashier-less stores. The systems are reliable when input images resemble training data but fail on unusual angles, lighting or objects they have not seen before.

What is edge AI and why does it matter?

Edge AI means running AI models on a local device rather than sending data to a remote server. Your phone's face-unlock system is edge AI. Benefits include faster responses, no internet dependency, and better privacy. The challenge is that the best AI models are very large and do not fit on devices with limited computing power, so smaller specialised models are required.

Which AI subfield will have the most impact in the next decade?

Edge AI is likely to be the most significant. NLP gets more attention but its impact is mainly cultural - chat and text generation. Computer vision is already embedded in real industrial systems. Edge AI - getting efficient models running on billions of devices - is foundational. Every phone, car, sensor and piece of industrial equipment could have local AI capabilities without cloud dependency.

How It Works

NLP systems use transformer architectures - the same foundation as GPT and Claude - to learn statistical relationships between words and phrases from billions of text examples. Given a prompt, the model predicts the most likely next token based on those learned patterns. This produces text that looks like understanding even though no understanding is happening.

Computer vision systems use convolutional neural networks (CNNs) that learn to detect edges, textures, shapes, and eventually objects by processing images through layers of filters. Each layer detects increasingly abstract features. The final layer produces a classification or detection output. See the deep learning lesson for detail on how this training works.

Edge AI typically uses model compression techniques - pruning (removing less important weights), quantisation (reducing numerical precision), and distillation (training a small model to mimic a large one) - to make models small enough to run on limited hardware without losing too much accuracy.

Key Points
  • NLP handles human language and powers ChatGPT, voice assistants, search, and email tools - but can't verify truth and is weaker in non-English languages
  • Computer vision handles images and video and is already embedded in medical, automotive, retail, and manufacturing systems
  • Both NLP and computer vision fail on inputs that don't resemble their training data - this is a fundamental limit, not just a bug to fix
  • Computer vision systems can be fooled by adversarial examples - tiny image modifications invisible to humans that completely mislead the AI
  • Edge AI runs models locally on devices, removing cloud dependency and improving privacy and speed
  • The main barrier to edge AI is model size - the best models are too large for most edge hardware, driving research into model compression
